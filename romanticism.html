Between the 18th and 19th century war is constantly raging across the world. While wars were fought, the literacy rate and amount of educated people increased due to industrial advances. Educated people who were forced to fight in wars questioned why they were fighting. People wanted a deeper understanding of themselves and how their life plays a role in society - they wanted to appreciate life’s subtle beauties conveyed through nature and emotion. The enlightenment inspired many people to begin thinking for themselves instead of following a figure head. This spearheaded the way for the romanticism era.

Romanticism literary writings were chalk full of sentimentalism; early stages of romanticism literature displayed spiritual connection between the individual and supernatural world. Writer like Friedrich Schlegel, Friedrich Hölderlin, and Johann Wolfgang von Goethe paved the way in mystical and psychedelic pieces. Friedrich Holderlin in particular wrote a piece called The Archipelago which highlights the theme of exile and divinity. In addition, literary focus shifted from religion and kingdoms to nationalism. A sense of pride developed in people for their nation of origin.

Poems in the romanticism era pertained the theme of sublimed individuals, respect towards nature, and imaginative. In romanticism, the poet’s sense logic is thrown out the door, and the mind is flooded with emotion written with every stroke of the pen on paper. In John Keats’ “To Hope” expresses his feelings of the battle of Waterloo and his mood towards Napoleon Bonaparte savage, war hungry quest.
